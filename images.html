Pictures of the Fair




The Midway Plaisance
Located along the southern edge of the fairgrounds, the Midway was a vibrant entertainment zone with exotic exhibits, amusements, and cultural displays. It featured attractions like the Ferris Wheel, a replica of a Cairo street, and various sideshows, giving visitors a chance to experience the world’s diversity and entertain themselves with thrilling rides.

The White City
The White City, the central area of the fair, featured traditional architecture and symbolized American progress and industry. The buildings, constructed in white cement, were designed by famous architects like Daniel Burnham and featured grand, classical facades. The area was home to the fair’s most impressive structures, including the first Ferris Wheel.

State Buildings
Each state in the U.S. had its own pavilion at the fair, showcasing its resources, culture, and contributions to the nation. These buildings were designed to reflect each state's character, featuring displays on agriculture, industry, and local history. Displayed below in order are pictures of the Missouri State Building, the Pennsylvania State Building, the Michigan State Building, the Illinois State Building, the California State Building, and the New York State Building.

Foreign Buildings
Each foreign nation at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition had its own pavilion, showcasing its culture, art, and innovations. These buildings were designed to reflect each country's architectural style. These exhibits highlighted the nation's achievements in areas such as industry, agriculture, and science. The countries with their own building included France, Italy, Germany, Japan, Britain, Egypt. Japan's building is displayed to the right.



Main Buildings of the 1893 World's Fair
The Administration Building, designed by Richard Morris Hunt, served as the central office of the fair with its iconic dome.
The Palace of Fine Arts, designed by Charles B. Atwood, showcased art and is now the Museum of Science and Industry.
The Manufactures and Liberal Arts Building, designed by George B. Post, was the largest building at the fair, displaying global goods.
The Agricultural Building, designed by McKim, Mead & White, displayed farming tools and even had a giant fruit-made Liberty Bell.
The Transportation Building, designed by Louis Sullivan, stood out with its colorful design and famous “Golden Door.”
Machinery Hall, designed by Peabody & Stearns, housed engines and machines that powered the fair.
The Fisheries Building, designed by Henry Ives Cobb, featured live fish displays and bright decorative tiles.
The Electricity Building, designed by Charles B. Atwood, showed off new electrical inventions and amazing light displays.

Natural Features of the Fair
The natural features of the fair were designed by landscape architect Frederick Law Olmsted. The fairgrounds featured lagoons, gardens, and the Wooded Island, which gave people a peaceful place to go while the big loud fair was happening. These natural features were designed to go with the architecture of the fair to make it nice to look at too.
